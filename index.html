Persuasive or Deceptive
Visualization?

Proposition

Clinic deserts don't reduce abortion rates, they force women to cross state lines.

For

the Proposition

States with the fewest clinics see the highest rates of women crossing state lines to seek care elsewhere. Source: Guttmacher Institute, 2020.

Design Decisions and Rationale

Score: −2 = fully deceptive  ·  0 = neutral  ·  +2 = fully earnest

  • −1

    Title phrased as a conclusion, not a description

    The visualization states "Clinic deserts don't reduce abortion rates, they force women to cross state lines," which is our proposition rather than a neutral title. Readers primed by this framing are more likely to interpret the bars as evidence for that claim before examining the data critically. A more neutral alternative title ("Out-of-state abortion travel by state, 2020") would be more earnest. We chose this charged title because it leads the reader to examine the visual from this conclusion, rather than creating one themselves.

  • −1

    Color-coded urgency tiers ("Extreme," "High," "Moderate")

    We assigned deep red to states where more than 50% of residents travel out of state, labeling this tier "Extreme." The warm red palette and loaded vocabulary carry emotional weight that nudges readers toward shock and alarm. At first, we considered a sequential color scheme, but landed on this to enact an idea of urgency. It was deliberately chosen to maximize impact without the deception being immediately obvious, as the actual data is correct, but the visual of color and language add emphasis to the narrative we are pushing.

  • −0.5

    Selective subset: top 15 states only

    Showing only the top 15 states by out-of-state travel excludes the many states with low travel rates, as showing all 50 would dilute the visual message. All 15 bars are high enough to look alarming at a glance. Showing all 50 states would be more complete and less deceptive, but when looking at the pattern with all 50 states, the impact was diminished.

  • +1.5

    Travel-centric metric captures the full picture

    Choosing "% of residents who traveled out of state" as the primary metric is our most earnest decision. It directly tests whether clinic deserts reduce overall abortion demand or simply displace it, and when looking at in-state rates alone, we missed the full picture. We considered several metrics such as total abortions, providers per capita, and rate per 1,000 women, but settled on the travel rate because it is the most complete answer to our proposition's claim.

Against

the Proposition

States where more women live in clinic deserts (low access to clinic) consistently report lower abortion rates. There's a downward trend, with blue high-access states clustered in the upper left and red low-access states in the lower right, suggesting that restricting access reduces the number of abortions that occur. Source: Guttmacher Institute, 2020.

Design Decisions and Rationale

Score: −2 = fully deceptive  ·  0 = neutral  ·  +2 = fully earnest

  • −2

    Omission of out-of-state travel data

    The scatter plot uses only in-state abortion occurrence rates, completely excluding cross-state travel. This is our most deceptive choice: a lower in-state rate looks like fewer abortions overall, but completely omits the idea that people can travel out of state if there are no clinics available. We were fully aware of this omission, and is why we landed on this opposition and visualization. We scored this −2 because it directly suppresses the most important alternative explanation for the downward pattern shown, and filters information that is crucial for a complete understanding.

  • −1.5

    Trendline and slope annotation imply causation

    The slope is mathematically valid, but we decided to annotate it in a way that implies a causal mechanism. The numerical language makes the relationship feel like a true outcome rather than a mere correlation.

  • −1

    Dark dashboard aesthetic signals scientific rigor

    Styling the chart with a near-black background, white gridlines, monochromatic dot colors, and a courier-style monospaced font for annotations allows us to mimic the aesthetic of professional and authoritative data visualizations and journalism. This visual language primes readers to trust the analysis as objective and rigorous. A plain white chart style would have presented the same correlation far less authoritatively, so we chose the dark theme specifically because it seemed to raise the perceived trustworthiness, credibility, and professional standing of the visualization.

  • −0.5

    Three-color dot encoding reinforces the narrative arc

    Coloring points by access tier (blue for high access, gray for moderate, red-orange for low) visually separates the scatter into three distinct clusters that follow the trendline downward. This makes the relationship look structurally clean and group-driven rather than noisy and overlapping. It also helped to separate the groups into colors to further exemplify the trendline downward, as without the colors and line, we noticed that some of the states had the same number of abortions per 1,000 women. By adding separated colors and trendline, we could shift the reader's focus on the correlation trend rather than individual states.

Final Reflection

The most striking realization from this project was how naturally deception emerges from choices that feel entirely defensible by itself. Selecting a meaningful metric, fitting a trendline, writing a title that "summarizes" the chart are all a part of creating a visualization. But when every choice is made in service of a predetermined conclusion, the cumulative effect misleads even when no individual step involves fabricating data. The AGAINST visualizations were the clearest illustration of this, as none of the techniques we used are individually dishonest, but when combining them together, alongside wanting to prove a specific narrative, it became more dishonest and desceptive.

We found the FOR visualizations easier to defend ethically because the travel metrics offer a more complete picture of what happens when clinic access is restricted. Even so, we made choices that were rhetorically loaded: labeling tiers "Extreme," writing the title as a declarative conclusion, and restricting the bar chart to the top 15 states that tell the most alarming story. The line between "choosing the right frame" and "manipulating the reader" proved narrower than we expected. Something that surprised us was that the FOR side, an idea in which we believe is more factually complete, still required deliberate rhetorical choices to be persuasive. It showed us that even the most honest visualizations may need to be carefully crafted to be effective.

We now understand that ethical visualization is less fixed and more like a practice of accountability. An acceptable persuasive choice, in our view, is one where the designer could transparently explain their decision, like including what was omitted and why, without the visualization losing credibility or information that leads to a more complete picture. A misleading choice is one whose persuasive power depends on the reader not noticing it. By that standard, omitting out-of-state travel from abortion rate charts is extremely misleading, while using urgency-signaling colors is merely opinionated. The practical boundary lies in whether the core empirical picture is intact or has been quietly replaced.
